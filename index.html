The Mental Health Landscape

Mental health affects millions of Australians every year. In 2020-2022, approximately 43% of Australians aged 16-85 experienced a mental disorder at some point in their lifetime. But the story varies significantly across our states and territories.

This visualization explores three critical questions:

  • Where are mental health challenges most prevalent?
  • Who is most affected, and are they getting help?
  • What happens when access to care is limited?

Where: Mental Health Across States and Territories

The map below shows the prevalence of 12-month mental disorders across Australian states and territories. Darker colors indicate higher rates of mental health conditions.

Key Insight: Mental health prevalence varies across regions, with some states showing significantly higher rates than others. Understanding these geographic patterns helps identify where resources are most needed.

Who: Age and Gender Patterns

Mental health doesn't affect everyone equally. Age and gender play significant roles in both prevalence and the types of disorders experienced.

Key Insight: Young adults (16-24 years) show the highest rates of mental health conditions, particularly anxiety disorders. Women report higher rates of anxiety and affective disorders, while men show higher rates of substance use disorders.

When Help Isn't Accessible: Understanding Outcomes

The most severe outcome of untreated mental health conditions is suicide. By examining suicide rates alongside service access, we can identify critical gaps in our mental health system.

Key Insight: There is a clear inverse relationship between service access and suicide rates. The Northern Territory, with the lowest service access rate (38%), has the highest suicide rate (21.7 per 100,000). In contrast, the ACT with the highest access rate (51%) has the lowest suicide rate (9.4 per 100,000). This demonstrates the critical importance of accessible mental health care.

What This Tells Us

The data reveals a clear narrative: mental health challenges are widespread across Australia, but access to care is not.

Key takeaways:

  • Mental health conditions affect people across all states and territories, but prevalence varies by region
  • Young adults are particularly vulnerable, with the highest rates of anxiety and affective disorders
  • Many people with mental health conditions do not access professional help
  • Regions with limited service access show concerning outcomes, including higher suicide rates

The path forward: Improving access to mental health services, particularly in underserved regions and for young people, is essential for better outcomes across Australia.
